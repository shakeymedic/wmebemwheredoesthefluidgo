Where Does the Fluid Go?

An Infographic Summary of Fluid Distribution for Emergency Medicine Doctors

Fluid Overload: A Balancing Act

Liberal crystalloid administration is common in EM, but both over- and under-hydration cause harm. A positive balance of 10-18L in a week is associated with a 50% mortality in post-op ARDS. So, where does all the excess fluid actually go?

Primary Accumulation Sites

  • Skin: High compliance for volume expansion. Impairs wound healing.
  • Intestinal Wall: Fenestrated capillaries allow rapid fluid transfer. Prolongs ileus.
  • Lungs: Leads to pulmonary oedema & ARDS. Mechanical ventilation worsens this by reducing lymphatic drainage.

A Three-Compartment Kinetic Model

Volume kinetic analysis shows crystalloid sequentially expands three functional compartments, not just two.

A: Fluid infuses into the Central space (Plasma, Vc), equilibrates with a fast-exchange interstitial pool (Vt1), and then a slow-exchange pool (Vt2). B & C: After a 1.9L Ringer's bolus, Vt2 opens and accumulates fluid, which persists long after plasma volume (Vc) has returned towards baseline.

The "Third Space" is Real: Meet Vt2

The slow-exchange interstitial space (Vt2) acts as an overflow reservoir. It prevents immediate intravascular fluid overload but prolongs total body oedema.

The Trigger Point:

Vt2 begins to fill rapidly after infusing approx. 1.3 - 1.5 Litres of crystalloid over 30 minutes. This corresponds to the point where interstitial pressure rises to zero, allowing "pitting oedema" to form.

Sepsis & Inflammation: A Vicious Cycle

In inflammatory states, cytokines create a "suction" effect in the interstitium, actively pulling fluid from the fast-exchange space (Vt1) into the slow space (Vt2).

The Pathophysiological Triad:

  • This suction reduces lymphatic flow.
  • This causes hypovolaemia and hypoalbuminaemia.
  • All while peripheral oedema worsens.
